# Why trusted origin matters

Origin claims affect market access, fraud risk, public trust, and cultural value, which is why provenance needs to be understandable to everyday users as well as auditors and developers.

!!! info "Research-backed explainer"
    This page synthesizes public research and sector guidance to explain the problem that Terroir is trying to address.

## The problem behind the promise

Traceability is not valuable because it sounds modern. It matters because producers, importers, regulators, and consumers frequently have to make decisions with incomplete or non-verifiable evidence. Once a label is easy to imitate and hard to audit, the most careful producers lose the ability to distinguish themselves.

Official guidance from the food-systems world treats traceability as a practical capability: it supports recalls, accountability, and chain visibility.[1][2] In high-value categories, anti-counterfeit work makes the same point from a different angle: when fraudulent goods circulate globally, trustworthy origin evidence becomes part of risk management rather than just marketing.[3]

## Why origin claims deserve special care

### Why the same data matters to different people

| Stakeholder | Why origin matters | What goes wrong without proof |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Producer | Origin supports price, reputation, and market differentiation. | Authentic work becomes indistinguishable from imitation. |
| Certifier | Origin is often tied to inspection scope and compliance rules. | Paper or siloed records slow verification and weaken enforcement. |
| Importer or buyer | Origin affects purchasing risk and downstream customer trust. | Disputes increase and due-diligence costs stay high. |
| Everyday consumer | Origin helps people connect price and story to evidence. | The buyer has to trust branding alone. |

For Georgian wine in particular, origin is not just a shipping fact. It carries archaeological, regional, and cultural significance, including the qvevri winemaking tradition highlighted by UNESCO.[4]

## What trustworthy origin should feel like

### Trust design principles

Terroir documentation uses these principles to explain the platform concept without assuming a specialized technical audience.

1. **Readable first** - A buyer or consumer should understand the claim before they need technical context.

2. **Evidence linked** - Important claims should point to who made them and what record supports them.

3. **Tampering visible** - If key records change later, that should be detectable instead of invisible.

4. **Portable across actors** - The record should survive handoffs between producers, certifiers, exporters, and buyers.
